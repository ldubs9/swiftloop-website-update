AI Agents Trained on Your Knowledge Base (RAG, Explained Simply)

What does it mean to train an AI on your own business? RAG explained in plain language — and why it stops AI from making things up.

A general AI model is brilliant and confidently wrong about your business. It has never seen your catalogue, your prices or your policies — so when asked, it guesses. For a company, a confident guess is worse than no answer. The fix has a name: RAG.

RAG in one sentence

Retrieval-augmented generation means: before the AI answers, it retrieves the real facts from your own content, then generates a reply based on those facts. Look it up, then answer — instead of answering from memory.

The library analogy

Imagine a brilliant assistant with no knowledge of your company. On their own, they'd improvise. Now give them a perfectly organised filing cabinet of everything true about your business. Ask a question and they walk to the right drawer, read the real document, and answer from it. RAG is that filing cabinet, wired into the AI.

Why it matters

  • No hallucinations — the agent answers from your facts, not its imagination.
  • Always current — update a document, and the agent's knowledge updates with it. No retraining.
  • Yours, specifically — it knows your stock, your shipping rules, your warranty, your tone.
  • Sourced — a good agent can tell you where an answer came from, so you can trust it.

What we feed it

Your product catalogue, FAQs, policies, past support conversations, manuals — whatever holds the truth about your business. The cleaner and more organised that knowledge, the sharper the agent. (Part of our job is helping you structure it.)

Where it lives

The same trained brain can power a chatbot on your website, a WhatsApp concierge, an internal assistant your team queries, or a voice agent on the phone. Train once, deploy everywhere.

The takeaway

A generic chatbot talks. A RAG agent knows. For any business where a wrong answer costs trust — luxury, finance, anything high-value — that difference is the whole point.

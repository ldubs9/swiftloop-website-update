# Web Accessibility and Why It Matters for Premium Brands

Accessibility — building a site that works for people with visual, motor, hearing or cognitive differences — is often treated as a legal box to tick. That framing undersells it. For a premium brand, accessibility is a mark of craft, a wider audience, and a reputation worth protecting. And much of it overlaps with simply building well.

## What it actually involves

- **Sufficient colour contrast** so text is readable for everyone, including the ageing eyes of an affluent clientele.
- **Keyboard navigability** so the site works without a mouse.
- **Screen-reader support** — proper structure and labels so assistive technology can describe the page.
- **Clear, legible typography and generous targets** so buttons and links are easy to use.
- **Meaningful alt text** on images, describing them for those who can't see them.

## Why premium brands especially should care

**It's craft.** A meticulously built, accessible site reflects the same attention to detail your products promise. Sloppy accessibility quietly contradicts a luxury positioning.

**It widens the audience.** A meaningful share of any audience has some access need. Excluding them — including wealthy, older buyers with declining vision — is leaving money and goodwill on the table.

**It protects reputation.** A brand publicly called out for an unusable site takes a reputational hit that far outweighs the cost of doing it right.

**It overlaps with quality.** Accessible sites tend to be cleaner, faster and better-structured — which also helps SEO and AI search. Good accessibility and good engineering pull in the same direction.

## The mindset

Accessibility isn't a feature you add at the end. It's a way of building from the start — considering everyone who'll use the site, not just the ideal visitor on a fast phone with perfect vision. That inclusive instinct is, itself, a sign of a serious brand.
